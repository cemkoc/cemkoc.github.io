For The Love of Coffee

19 October 2016 on Travels

Last winter, in the span of 27 days, I traveled to Asia and visited 6 different countries, rode on 12 different flights, ate tons of delicious food, and made many new friends. Here I am, many days after the trip, reflecting upon my trip. Here’s my best recollection of everything that happened each day.

I left San Jose International Airport near noontime onboard All Nippon Airways Flight 171, operated by a Boeing 787–800. Nothing noteworthy happened on this flight; I got a middle seat on the right section of the aircraft. Fast-forward 10 hours later, I’m in Narita!

After exchanging money at the airport, I headed downstairs towards the airport express train station. As I soon learned, Japanese people are extremely friendly towards others. I approached the station counter to buy a ticket towards Nippori station and after coughing over 2400¥ for my ticket, I headed downstairs to the platform. The train departed 10 minutes after I arrived and 36 minutes later, I was at Nippori station.

Before I had left Narita airport, I took screenshots of the path to my hotel, Hotel Lungwood. So, after a little difficulty placing myself on the map, I was able to head in the right direction. I overshot where I had to go by a block, which happened for several more times before I finally figured it out, I arrived at my hotel and checked in at around 5pm.

At around 6:30pm, I decided to leave my hotel and headed towards Shinjuku. I wanted to catch a glimpse of Tokyo at night at the Tokyo Metropolitan Government Building (free) and then also buy a data sim card at Yodabashi Camera, which my hotel front desk recommended to go to.

Walking to the Metropolitan from the subway station took about 10 minutes and after a quick elevator ride up to the 45th floor, I was able to indulge in the breathtaking views of Tokyo!

Upon leaving the Tokyo Metropolitan, I headed towards the shops near Shinjuku station. Initially, I found a store that had Yodobashi written in Kanji (after character matching, which I had to do again in Korea), but, walking inside, I saw nothing more than lots of console games, anime, Nitendo, etc. A store clerk, who spoke very little English, directed me to another store in the same district for my sim cards and I bought my sim cards there for about 1900¥.

By this time, I was getting pretty hungry. Yet, at the same time, I was quite nervous on walking in to a restaurant and figuring out how to order. After all, what’s there to be afraid of?

After walking up and down the streets looking for my first Japanese restaurant to dine at, I settled on a cute little ramen shop. The waiters/chef greeted me as a I walked and pointed towards the ticketing machine. They realized I was clueless as to how to use it and one of them helped me order my first ramen from the machine. I just picked the one that had the nicest picture.

Of course, the ramen wasn’t dispensed from the machine. My friends thought I had bought instant ramen on my first day in Japan. After ordering and paying at the machine, the waiter takes the order and the chef makes it. I guess it saves the restaurant workers from ever touching dirty money, which I suspect isn’t really that dirty in Japan. After all, Japan was so clean!

The ramen cost about 900¥ and it was absolutely delicious! My stomach grumbles at the sight of the picture above. The noodles, the soup, the pork loin — they were all on point. My only gripe? They didn’t give enough, especially the meat, which I realized was a trend all over Japan. Granted, I was judging Japanese cuisine under my American culturally indoctrinated platter portion expectations. Anyways, following my delicious meal, I took the metro back to my hotel and got some good rest.
